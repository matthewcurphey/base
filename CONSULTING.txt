This is the most common blocker for technical people and it's solvable. The good news: you don't need to be a networker. You need to have useful conversations with a small number of the right people.

Reframe it first

Networking as a concept is repellent because it implies schmoozing, collecting contacts, working a room. Forget that. What you're actually doing is having conversations with people who have a problem, to understand it better. You're not selling anything. You're learning. That's a completely different energy and most people will talk to you.

Where the people actually are

LinkedIn is the answer for B2B. Specifically:

CFOs, COOs, Operations Directors, Finance Directors at mid-market manufacturing companies (50-500 employees)
"Head of Operations" at PE-backed businesses
Operating partners at PE firms (their job is literally fixing portcos — they're always looking for people who can do what you do)
Search: Operations Director manufacturing or CFO [city] manufacturing or operating partner private equity. Filter to 2nd connections first — warm is easier.

What to actually say

Cold outreach works if it's short, specific, and not salesy. This is the template:

"Hi [name] — I'm an ops guy who's spent the last few years building data infrastructure to connect multiple ERPs at a manufacturing business. I'm exploring whether this is a wider problem worth solving. Not selling anything — genuinely trying to understand if other businesses hit the same walls we do. Would you be up for a 20-min call?"

That's it. No pitch deck. No "I'd love to connect and discuss synergies." Just a specific, honest ask from one ops person to another.

Response rate on this done right: maybe 10-20%. You need to send 20 messages to get 3-4 conversations. That's fine.

The easier on-ramp: warm contacts you don't realise you have

Before cold outreach, mine what you already have:

Former colleagues who've moved to other companies — even one or two from your career
People your current colleagues know that you could get introduced to
Anyone you've ever dealt with professionally outside your firm: suppliers, customers, advisors, auditors
A warm intro converts at 60-70% vs 10-20% cold. Even a weak tie ("my colleague used to work with you") transforms the conversation.

Message former colleagues first: "Hey — I'm exploring doing some consulting work around data/ops infrastructure. You've moved around a bit — do you know anyone at manufacturing businesses who might be dealing with ERP integration headaches? Happy to have a chat with them, no pressure."

People love making introductions. It makes them feel useful.

The baby + remote angle actually helps

You have a legitimate reason to be having these conversations right now without it looking like you're actively job hunting or pitching. "I'm on the side exploring whether this is a wider problem" is completely honest and low pressure. Nobody's guard goes up.

Concrete starting point

This week:

Go through your LinkedIn connections and write down every person who works in manufacturing, operations, finance, or PE — at any company that isn't yours. You probably have more than you think.
Pick 3. Send the message above adapted to what you know about them.
See what happens.
You don't need a network. You need 5 honest conversations. Start there.

What you're actually selling

"I connect your systems and give you one clean number you can trust."

That's it. You're not selling a data warehouse. You're not selling best practice architecture. You're selling the answer to "why does my ERP say 847 units and my spreadsheet says 912 and nobody knows which is right."

The thing you built at work — multi-ERP → Postgres → dbt → clean output — is genuinely what they need. Not Snowflake, not a data team, not a 6-month transformation project. A lightweight reporting layer that sits on top of what they already have and tells them what's going on.

Why the "fast and lightweight" angle is a feature

Mid-market operations directors don't want a data warehouse. They've probably had a bad experience with a consultant who promised transformation and delivered a 6-month project that never quite worked. You're the opposite of that:

No ripping out existing systems
No long implementation
Connects to what they have
Gives them one dashboard or report they actually use
Done in weeks not months
That's a much easier sell than "let's modernise your data infrastructure."

The specific problem statement

Frame it around the pain, not the solution:

"You've got two or three systems that don't talk to each other. Someone's maintaining a master spreadsheet that's always slightly wrong. You don't fully trust the numbers when you're in a board meeting. I fix that — I pull it all into one place and give you a single clean output. I don't touch your existing systems."

Every mid-market ops or finance person will nod at that description.

What the engagement actually looks like

Week 1-2: understand their systems, what data exists, what question they actually need answered
Week 3-6: build the connectors, the light transformation layer, the output (dashboard or Excel or both)
Ongoing: £2-3k/month retainer to maintain it, refresh it, add to it
The retainer is important. That's what makes it a business rather than a project. And it's genuinely justified — systems change, data changes, they'll want new reports.

You're not underselling yourself by keeping it simple

The temptation is to feel like "just a reporting layer" isn't sophisticated enough to charge real money for. It absolutely is. The sophistication is invisible to them — what they see is that the number is finally right. That's what they're paying for.

The move in the meantime

Don't sit completely idle on the business front. Use the time to:

Get the personal projects to a point where you can actually ship something
Have 2-3 low-pressure exploratory conversations (LinkedIn cold outreach) just to practice the pitch and understand what other companies actually struggle with
Build one more engagement's worth of knowledge — cloud deployment, dbt Cloud, maybe a simple dashboard layer
So when the exit happens and the CEO says "you should go talk to X" — you've already had the conversation a few times and you don't fumble it.

The exit conversation to have before it happens

When the exit starts moving, have an honest conversation with your CEO: "I've been thinking about going independent after this. Would you be willing to introduce me to a couple of people you know?"

CEOs love this. It costs them nothing, it's a favour to someone they rate, and it makes them look good. One warm intro from a credible CEO in the PE world is worth 50 cold LinkedIn messages.

You're not waiting — you're positioning.

Not the CFO at the portco. The operating partner or value creation lead at the PE firm itself. That's the person who sits across multiple portcos and has both the budget authority and the problem visibility. One relationship there = pipeline.
